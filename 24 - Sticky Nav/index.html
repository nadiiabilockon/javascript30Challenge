“Under the Forest Lights — A Short Story”

Jeremy was eight years old when he met her, and even then, she was the most beautiful girl he’d ever seen. When his dad got a new job outside the city, Jeremy moved into a new house with his mom and dad. The house sat on the edge of a forest that reminded him of something he read in a storybook. The trees were unnaturally colorful, with deep greens, bright reds, and even a touch of blue. It was the kind of forest that seemed almost magical.

Jeremy, against his parents’ judgment, decided to explore one evening. When he tripped on a tree root and scuffed his knee, a little girl his age appeared from behind a tree.

She looked at him strangely with her dark eyes. Her hair fell to her shoulders in loose curls. He followed her gaze to the spot on his knee that was covered in blood. Concern was written on her face, but she said nothing. She backed up slowly and turned to walk away.

“It’s okay,” Jeremy said. “It doesn’t really hurt that bad.”

The girl stopped and turned toward him, her eyes wide. “Are you talking to… me?” she said with a soft voice.

Jeremy pushed himself off the ground and wiped the blood of his knee with his hand. “I didn’t see anybody else out here. Do you live around here?”

She stepped toward him, her eyes taking on a calmer quality. Her mouth, which had before been wide open, formed into a smile. “Will you play with me?”

And so began their friendship. Allie lived with her family in a small house in a clearing in the middle of the forest. She was an only child. That day, they chased each other around trees and Jeremy barely noticed that the leaves of the trees glowed in the darkness, giving light to their play.

Though his mom was unhappy when he turned up at home well past dark, she smiled as he told her about his new friend and the fun they had together.

They played every day for two weeks until the evening he followed her to her house. It was an old but sturdy brick house.

“My mommy and daddy say I’m not supposed to bring anybody here,” Allie said.

“Why not?”

“I don’t know,” she said, her usual smile broken. “I tried to tell them that you’re my friend.” She looked at her front door. “They’re always sad.”

The door opened and a large man with a long beard of dark brown stepped out. At the sight of Jeremy, the man stopped. “Who are you?” He glanced out at the forest behind Jeremy. “What are you doing here?”

“Daddy, this is my friend,” Allie said.

Ignoring her, he stepped out into the yard, locking his gaze on Jeremy. A small woman with dark hair that matched the color of Allie’s stepped out behind him.

“I’m J-J-Jeremy.” Jeremy took a step back and looked at Allie.

“You have to go,” the man said. “You have to go now.”

“I’m sorry,” Jeremy said. “Allie said I wasn’t supposed to come here.”

The man looked at him, confused. “Allie…” he said.

The man’s wife pulled him back toward the house. “Allie, come inside,” she said before turning her gaze to Jeremy. “Just go,” she whispered.

He took one look at Allie, who had tears forming in the corners of her eyes, and ran as fast as his little legs would carry him.

He didn’t see her again for eight years. It wasn’t that he didn’t go into the forest again. He did, but she never showed up again, and he wasn’t willing to go near her house after the encounter with her parents. One evening, when he was sixteen, he was sitting on the back porch, furiously typing away on a story he was working on for the school newspaper. He noticed movement in the forest, and his skin began to tingle.

Allie? he thought.

Laying his laptop down, he made his way to the tree line, which was blocked by a chain-link fence by this time. In the distance, he saw her. She was older, of course, but the sight of her had a much more profound effect on him than it did when he was eight years old. He hopped the fence and walked toward her. Her dark hair still framed her face in loose curls, and her face still lit up with the same smile he remembered.

“Hi, Jeremy,” she said. “I hoped you would see me.”

He threw his arms around her and hugged her. “Where have you been? Why haven’t I seen you all this time?”

“I’m sorry,” she said, holding him tight. “My parents can get pretty protective.”

He let her go though he didn’t want to. “Your dad looked at me like I was a ghost that day.”

She laughed. “You kinda were,” she said. “We don’t see many people.” “Why don’t I see you at school?” They started walking deeper into the forest. The leaves of the trees gave off their glow. “Every year I’ve looked, hoping I’d see you.” “I’m homeschooled,” she said. She stopped and looked at him. “What’s it like to go to school?”

He spent the rest of the evening telling her about his classes, his favorite teacher Mr. Renier, who was encouraging him to be a journalist, his job on the school newspaper, and his best friend Asher, who he met because of a shared love of Christopher Nolan films when they were in sixth grade. She listened to him with rapt attention, hanging onto every detail.

He told her about his dream to move to the city after high school to study journalism, and she seemed to love this detail the most. “I wish I could see the city,” she said. “It sounds so fascinating.” “Let’s go together,” Jeremy said. “I can drive us.”

Her smile disappeared as she looked down at the ground. “I couldn’t do that,” she said. “My parents don’t even know I’m talking to you. They’d never let me go to the city.”

Even though he hadn’t seen her in years, it felt like they’d never been apart. Yet somehow, it also seemed like something critical about their relationship was changing as he took her hand in his. “It’s okay,” he said. “We can just stay here.” Her smile returned. “I missed you.” “I never stopped looking for you.” She squeezed his hand. “Well, I’m here now.” For the next year, they saw each other intermittently though it wasn’t nearly as often as Jeremy would’ve liked. Allie didn’t talk about her parents, but he suspected they kept her inside most of the time. He felt sorry for her. She was like a bird, forbidden to use her wings to fly. He wondered why her parents were so controlling. When they were together, Jeremy spoke often of his dream to return to Sadia, the city where he’d spent the first eight years of his life. He asked her to go with him to the city several times, but she insisted she couldn’t.

Several months before his eighteenth birthday, after they hadn’t seen each other for a while, they met at an outcropping they’d found on one of their earlier explorations. There was a bench where they often sat together, staring up at the stars. “Guess what,” she said, taking a seat next to him. Taking her hand, he scooted closer to her. “You’re finally going to go on a date with me to the city.” She laughed. “No, silly,” she said. “It’s my birthday.” His eyes met hers. “Why didn’t you tell me? I would’ve gotten you something.” He laughed. “I can’t believe after all this time, I’ve never known your birth date.” Allie smiled. “It’s okay. I guess I really don’t know yours either.” He told her. Not that it was important; just to be with her was enough of a gift for any day. He squeezed her hand. “So what shall we do for your birthday?” She stood. “There’s a place I want to show you.” Allie took him to a high hill he’d never been to before. As the sun began to set, he could make out the city with its bright lights and tall buildings in the distance. Nearing the edge of the hill, he realized Sadia Lake separated the forest from the city. He’d never realized the city was so close to home. The sight was breath-taking and he realized he was staring.

“That’s exactly what I wanted for my birthday,” Allie said, interrupting his thoughts. Jeremy met her gaze. “What?” She was smiling. “That look on your face,” she said. “I know how much you love the city. When I found this place, I knew I had to bring you here.” She pulled close to him and laid her head on his shoulder. He kissed the top of her head, taking in the softness of her hair and the smell of her perfume. “It’s incredible,” he said. He held her tight as he looked out on the ripples of white from the moon and the city lights reflected on the water. It would only be a year before he went to the city, but looking at it now with Allie inspired a new hope in his mind. He took her hand in his. “I love you, Allie,” he said. It was the first time he’d said it. Her face lit up. “I love you too.” He wasn’t finished. “I can’t imagine going to the city next year without you.” She stepped back from him, a sudden look of concern on her face. “What are you saying?” He pulled close to her again, taking both of her hands in his. “I want to spend the rest of my life with you. Marry me. We’ll go to the city together.” She pulled her hands away. “I can’t, Jeremy.” He couldn’t believe it. “You just said you love me too.”

“I do. More than you know.”

“Then why?”

She walked to the edge of the hill and he joined her. “I’m dying,” she said as she gazed out at the water. Her words cut him like a blade to the heart. She turned to him with a look of deep sadness in her eyes. “I don’t have much time left,” she said in a broken voice.

“Why didn’t you tell me? How long have you known?” His heart felt like a pounding drum in his chest. “I didn’t want you to see me differently. I didn’t want our relationship to be defined by you wanting to fight for me.” He grabbed her and pulled her close. Tears filled his eyes as his body began to tremble. “There has to be a chance,” he said, his voice just as broken as hers. “I can’t let you die.” She pushed away from him and looked deeply into his eyes. For a moment she said nothing, as if she were searching for the right words. “My doctors say I won’t survive beyond my eighteenth birthday. There’s nothing they can do.” He didn’t know what to say. She had maybe a year left, and then she would be gone. He’d spent the best parts of the last decade just being with her. He couldn’t imagine life without her. “Don’t give up on your dream, Jeremy,” Allie said, her voice tinged with more conviction. “Not for me. Not for anybody.” She put her hand to his cheek. “Go to the city. Get married. Start a family. Be happy.” It was too much. He cleared his throat, fighting back the tears. “I just want you.” Allie’s face twisted as her confidence broke. “I’m so sorry.” She said it as if it were her fault. “I just want you to live.” They spent the rest of the night huddled together in silence, watching the city come alive in the darkness. The next several months passed quickly. Jeremy worried that he’d lose Allie any day, but she seemed certain that she’d survive until her eighteenth birthday, and then no longer. Several times he asked her to marry him anyway and move to the city. Senior year was coming to a close and he had until August before he would be leaving, which would be one month after her birthday. They could leave early, he told her, and move into an apartment in May. Though she loved the thought, she insisted it wouldn’t work and she didn’t want to bring that level of unhappiness to him when she finally passed.

The day arrived, and Jeremy couldn’t make it to Allie fast enough. He ran through the forest, which was unusually lit up for the morning. He made it to her house, which was quiet. In ten years, he’d never been inside. He waited for her, expecting her to come out any minute. But minutes turned into an hour and he felt the weight that he was running out of time. It had been ten years since he encountered her parents, and the look on her dad’s face that day still haunted him. But he had to see her. He walked up to the front door and tentatively knocked on the door. No one answered. He tapped on the door with more force, but still nothing. “Allie!” He pounded on the door. “Please! I just need to see her!” The door opened, and her dad, older and with a deranged look on his face, peeked his head out. “Please,” the man said. “You can’t be here.”

“I just want to see Allie. I just need to know she’s okay.” “Allie’s not here,” the man replied. “Is it the boy?” he heard an older woman’s voice inside say.

“Please just go,” the man said. “I don’t know how you think you know Allie, but that’s impossible.” Jeremy couldn’t make sense of the man’s words. Why was he acting like Allie didn’t know him? The man moved to close the door, and Jeremy, in desperation, pushed his way through. “I have to see her,” he said as he forced his way into the house. The man fell back and Allie’s mom stood still, afraid. Jeremy put his hands up. “I’m not here to hurt anybody,” he said. “I’m just worried about Allie.” The woman pointed a frail finger toward a bedroom behind Jeremy. “She’s in there.” Jeremy walked into the room, and the sight nearly broke him. Allie was on a bed, unconscious. Her skin was light, and her body was thinner than he’d ever seen it. A strange glowing blue light hovered next to her body. He approached her and took her hand. “Why is she like this?” The man and his wife stepped into the room. The man spoke first. “She’s always been like this.”

Jeremy looked back at the man, confusion setting in. “What do you mean?” This time the woman spoke. “Our daughter’s been lying in that bed for the past eighteen years. Ever since she was born.” “That’s impossible. I’ve been with her hundreds of times.” The man pulled up an old rickety chair from the corner of the room and took a seat. He sighed before launching into the story that would change Jeremy’s life. “Before Allie was born,” the man began, “we were told by a mysterious man that she would be born with a broken heart. With someone else’s heart. Her heart would reside in someone who was meant to die so that she would give life.” He pointed to the blue light. “The forest lights are the only thing keeping her alive. The heart inside of her failed her long ago. The forest lights could only keep her alive until today.” The man’s chin dropped as he was clearly overcome with a hopelessness Jeremy was only beginning to know. “Why can’t she have a heart transplant?” he said.

Allie’s mom stepped forward and placed her hand on his shoulder. The gesture was surprisingly calming. “Allie’s heart isn’t like any normal heart,” she said. “The man told us her heart, the one that resides in someone else, is the only heart her body will accept. My husband and I would gladly give our hearts if it would save her.” Her voice fell quiet. “Our hearts won’t save her.” Jeremy stood. “She can’t die,” he said, indignant. “Someone out there has her heart. That’s not fair. How can you even know who has her heart?” The man and the woman looked at each other, a look of debate in their eyes. “How do we stop this?” Jeremy insisted.

The man cleared his throat. “The man who told us about the forest lights and about Allie’s heart told us that Allie’s spirit would know who her heart belonged to. He said that the person with her heart would see her spirit in the forest as if she were standing in front of them.” The truth slammed into him with enough force to cause him sit down again. “It’s me. Isn’t it?” He remembered the first time he saw her. The way she looked at him, as if he shouldn’t be able to see her. He remembered her touch, the way her lips felt when he kissed them. She’d been so real. “She knew,” he said. “Why didn’t she tell me?”

“I’ve seen her at times,” her mother said. “Something about being her mom, I guess.” The woman smiled. “You’ve made her happy. She truly loves you.” Jeremy looked at Allie’s face, which looked so peaceful. He thought of his dreams of moving to the city. Thoughts that were quickly broken by Allie’s voice saying how much she wished she could go to the city. He imagined her walking through the streets of Sadia, pushing a stroller, being happy. It was her heart beating inside him. They were meant for each other, and yet they could never be together. He put his hand on his chest to feel the beat of Allie’s heart.

He knew what he had to do. He bent down and kissed her cheek. “Thanks for giving me your heart, Allie,” he whispered. “Now it’s your turn.”
